Frequently Asked Technical Questions

Understanding the technology behind ChemSynth AI.

How does the AI model actually predict reactions?

Our service uses a state-of-the-art T5 Transformer model. It treats chemistry as a "language translation" task. We convert your input reactants and reagents into a specific text format, for example: "reactants>CCO.O=C(O)C>products>". The AI model, having been trained on millions of reaction examples, then predicts the most statistically likely sequence of characters to complete that sentence, which in this case would be the SMILES string for the product.

What is a SMILES string?

SMILES (Simplified Molecular-Input Line-Entry System) is a standard method for representing a molecule's structure using a simple line of text. For example, ethanol is written as CCO, and benzene is c1ccccc1. It is the language our AI model reads and writes.

Why are some of my predictions invalid or messy?

This is a great question. The AI is probabilistic, not a calculator. It predicts what is statistically likely, but it doesn't have a true understanding of chemical rules. Sometimes it "hallucinates" and generates a chemically impossible structure or includes leftover ions (like [Cl-]). Our application's code is designed to intelligently filter these outputs, splitting them into parts and only showing you the valid, organic molecules.

What is RDKit and what is its role here?

RDKit is an open-source cheminformatics toolkit. In our application, its primary job is to act as the "renderer." After our AI model predicts a product as a SMILES string, we pass that valid string to RDKit. RDKit then translates the text into the 2D molecular image that you see on the screen. The "RDKit could not parse..." error appears when the AI provides a SMILES string that is not chemically or syntactically valid.

What data was the model trained on?

The model (sagawa/ReactionT5v2-forward-USPTO_MIT) was fine-tuned on the USPTO-MIT dataset. This is a massive collection of chemical reactions extracted from U.S. patents. This is a key advantage, as it means the model has learned from real-world, commercially relevant reactions, not just simple textbook examples.

Can I trust the predictions 100%?

The model has a very high top-1 accuracy of 97.5% on its test dataset. However, it should be used as a powerful hypothesis generation tool—an expert assistant to suggest likely outcomes. It dramatically accelerates research by pointing you in the right direction, but all AI-generated predictions should be reviewed with sound chemical principles before being attempted in a lab.
